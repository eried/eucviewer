Distance per group & cumulative lifetime

Each bar is how much you rode in that group. The line is your total distance so far. Empty bars mean you didn't ride in that period.

PATTERN Trip-distance distribution

How long are your rides? (trip count by distance band)

Shows how many of your rides land in each distance band. Tells you whether you mostly do short hops, long cruises, or both.

EVIDENCE+WEATHER Estimated full range

Range over time (median per group)
Range vs ambient temperature WEATHER (one dot per trip)
Range vs average riding speed (one dot per trip)
In-ride cost of speed (full-charge range by riding speed band)

Every ride is cut into short slices, and each slice lands in the speed band it was ridden at. Each bar shows how far a full charge would take you if the whole ride stayed in that band. Unlike the scatter above, this compares speeds inside your rides, so route mix can't skew it. Slow bands cost more because balancing and stop-and-go burn power while covering almost no distance. Fast bands cost more because air drag grows with the square of speed. The cheapest cruise is the green band in between.

Range vs climb rate (meters of altitude gain per km of ride)

For every trip we compute distance / battery used × 100 to estimate how far you'd have gone on a full charge. The dot is the typical value for that group (weighted by distance), the shaded band shows the middle half of the trips (25th to 75th percentile). Trips that used less battery than the threshold above are skipped. If your logger keeps writing junk after the wheel turns off (frozen voltage and battery), we trim that tail before measuring.

EVIDENCE Charging events

Charges per group (detected from battery jumps between rides)
State of charge per top-up (start → end %)

We detect a charge whenever your next trip starts with a higher battery than the previous one ended at. Topping up from a high state of charge (say 80% to 100%) is easier on lithium-ion packs than charging from deep discharges (say 15% to 100%), which ages them faster.

EVIDENCE Top speed

Top speed over time (max + median per group, GPS-verified)
Speed distribution across rides (trip count by top-speed band)

We compare every wheel-speed sample against GPS speed. Samples where the wheel is spinning but the GPS shows it didn't actually move (a pickup test or fall) are removed, so a lifted wheel can't fake your top speed.

FINDING Acceleration

Best 0 → 40 km/h time per group (seconds, lower is faster)

For every ride we look for runs where you accelerated from a near-stop up to 40 km/h (or 25 if you rarely hit 40, 60 when you do). Free-spin samples and physically impossible accelerations are skipped, so a lifted wheel can't fake a fast launch. Shorter times mean the wheel has more punch on tap, which is a useful signal for spotting pack sag as it ages.

DIAGNOSTIC Wheel speed vs GPS speed

Typical disagreement over time (median |wheel − GPS| per group, km/h)
GPS lag over time (seconds GPS arrives behind the wheel reading)
Difference distribution (per-trip mean |wheel − GPS|)

For every sample where you're actually moving and the GPS has a fix, we compare what the wheel says you're doing against what the GPS says you're doing.

Disagreement: the typical gap between the two readings. Lower is better. A small constant gap usually means the wheel sensor is mis-scaled (tire wear, wrong wheel size in the firmware). A growing gap with speed means slip / dropouts.
Lag: we slide the GPS series back and forth across the wheel series and find the offset that lines them up best. Positive means GPS arrives that many seconds behind the wheel - usually true because GPS receivers smooth their output. The lag tightening over time means newer firmware / newer phone GPS is keeping up better.
Spikes: samples where the two readings disagreed by more than 5 km/h. Usually a tunnel / GPS dropout, occasionally wheel-side noise.

EVIDENCE G-Force

Peak G over time (per group: instantaneous and sustained for ≥ 1 s)
G-force distribution (peak |G| per trip)
Grip envelope: lateral G vs speed (one dot per high-G sample, old → new)

A raw G spike is usually just a pothole, a curb hit, or you picking the wheel up. We only count G that happened during a real maneuver:

Corner: GPS heading is rotating faster than 8 degrees per second for at least one second, and you were going faster than 15 km/h. The lateral G we keep is the centripetal force you held through the turn.
Hard brake: longitudinal G stayed at or below -0.15 for at least one second from over 15 km/h.
Launch: longitudinal G stayed at or above 0.15 for at least one second, starting from under 5 km/h.

The grip envelope plots average speed in each corner against the peak lateral G you held in that corner. The upper edge of the cloud is your friction envelope: how hard you can actually lean at each speed. If your logger doesn't have an IMU, we estimate centripetal G from speed × heading rate on the GPS track and mark the section as GPS-estimated.

ANOMALY Free spins & suspected falls

Detection thresholds i

Events fall into four kinds:
Lift: pickup test or hand-spin. GPS shows the wheel didn't move and the motor stayed idle.
Fall: you were genuinely cruising before, the wheel either freespun fast or peaked above riding speed, then GPS dropped to zero and stayed there. Slow-speed blips don't qualify.
Spike: brief sensor blip during normal riding. GPS dropout in a tunnel, momentary wheel-side noise. Not a real lift.
Glitch: cascade of phantom samples while the motor was idle for the whole surrounding window. Logger or firmware artifact, not a rider event.

Tune the thresholds above if your wheel triggers too many false positives or misses real events.

EVIDENCE Efficiency

Energy use over time (median per group)
Power-draw distribution (trip count by avg-W band)
Cost of standing still (battery per hour at walking-and-below paces)

Battery gauges bounce back up right after load disappears, so battery flow at low speeds is measured carefully: for the moving paces drops are netted against rebounds, and the standing-still number comes only from stops of 2 minutes or more with stable voltage, where the rebound has settled and charging breaks are excluded. Hover a bar for the time sampled and the per-distance equivalent.

Energy per distance (Wh/km, computed from logged power or voltage×current) and distance per battery percent (km/%). The histogram shows how hard you typically push the wheel: most riders sit in a narrow average-power band with a long tail toward steep climbs / sprints.

EVIDENCE Motor regen

Regen share over time (median %, regen Wh as fraction of drive Wh, per group)
Regen recovered vs descent (one dot per trip, old → new)
Regen-share distribution (trip count by regen %)

When you coast, brake, or descend, the wheel runs the motor as a generator. Sample-by-sample power: positive = drive, negative = motor regen. Regen share = regen Wh / drive Wh.

Caveat: the POWER column logs motor-side power, not net battery flow. Real EUC regen efficiency is 30 to 60%. The rest is heat in motor windings and FETs. So a "10% regen share" here is roughly 3 to 6% actual battery savings. Each trip's regen Wh is capped at its drive Wh, so one ride can't recover more than it used. Some regen shows on every trip because balance pulses constantly toggle motor torque direction.

EVIDENCE Motor: speed vs current

Avg speed vs avg current (one dot per trip, old → new)

Each dot is one trip. Average moving speed vs average positive current, coloured oldest (blue) to newest (red). If recent dots sit above older ones at the same speed, the drivetrain is drawing more amps than it used to.

Current efficiency over time (amps per unit of speed)

Median amps per km/h per group. A speed-independent view of the same drift, easier to read than the raw scatter.

EVIDENCE+WEATHER Thermal

Temperature rise is how much hotter the board got versus the air around you. Hot motors mean more electrical losses, more cooling pressure on the pack, and higher risk of thermal cutoff on long climbs.

How much hotter the wheel got WEATHER (per ride, vs power output)

Each dot is one ride. X is the average power the wheel pulled, Y is the peak temperature rise. Dots drift from blue (oldest) to red (newest). If recent (red) dots sit above older (blue) ones at the same power, the wheel is running hotter for the same work, a sign of degraded cooling or pack aging.

Heat-up per ride, tracked over time (median °C above ambient, per group)

Median rise per group across time. Flat line means consistent thermal behaviour. Drifting up means the wheel is getting hotter to do the same ride.

EVIDENCE Battery health: voltage sag

Two independent battery-aging proxies, one based on instantaneous load response, the other on full-ride peak vs trough voltage.

Effective internal resistance over time (median per group, mΩ)

Effective IR is the slope of voltage against current using only short-timestep deltas, so SoC drift across the trip cancels out. Rising IR over time means the pack is harder to push current through, a real aging signal.

Voltage droop over the trip (% sag from peak, lower is healthier)

Voltage droop is the gap between the pack's peak resting voltage and its lowest loaded voltage during the same ride. Naturally widens as the pack ages.
