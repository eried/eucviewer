EVIDENCE Riding activity

Distance per group & cumulative lifetime

Each bar is how much you rode in that group. The line is your total distance so far. Empty bars mean you didn't ride in that period.

PATTERN Trip-distance distribution

How long are your rides? (trip count by distance band)

Shows how many of your rides land in each distance band. Tells you whether you mostly do short hops, long cruises, or both.

EVIDENCE+WEATHER Estimated full range

Range over time (median per group)
Range vs ambient temperature WEATHER (one dot per trip)

For every trip we compute distance / battery used × 100 to estimate how far you'd have gone on a full charge. The dot is the typical value for that group (weighted by distance), the shaded band shows the middle half of the trips (25th to 75th percentile). Trips that used less battery than the threshold above are skipped. If your logger keeps writing junk after the wheel turns off (frozen voltage and battery), we trim that tail before measuring.

EVIDENCE Charging events

Charges per group (detected from battery jumps between rides)
State of charge per top-up (start → end %)

We detect a charge whenever your next trip starts with a higher battery than the previous one ended at. Topping up from a high state of charge (say 80% to 100%) is easier on lithium-ion packs than charging from deep discharges (say 15% to 100%), which ages them faster.

EVIDENCE Top speed

Top speed over time (max + median per group, GPS-verified)
Speed distribution across rides (trip count by top-speed band)

We compare every wheel-speed sample against GPS speed. Samples where the wheel is spinning but the GPS shows it didn't actually move (a pickup test or fall) are removed, so a lifted wheel can't fake your top speed.

FINDING Acceleration

Best 0 → 40 km/h time per group (seconds, lower is faster)

For every ride we look for runs where you accelerated from a near-stop up to 40 km/h (or 25 if you rarely hit 40, 60 when you do). Free-spin samples and physically impossible accelerations are skipped, so a lifted wheel can't fake a fast launch. Shorter times mean the wheel has more punch on tap, which is a useful signal for spotting pack sag as it ages.

EVIDENCE G-Force

Peak G over time (per group: instantaneous and sustained for ≥ 1 s)
G-force distribution (peak |G| per trip)
Grip envelope: lateral G vs speed (one dot per high-G sample, old → new)

A raw G spike is usually just a pothole, a curb hit, or you picking the wheel up. We only count G that happened during a real maneuver:

Corner: GPS heading is rotating faster than 8 degrees per second for at least one second, and you were going faster than 15 km/h. The lateral G we keep is the centripetal force you held through the turn.
Hard brake: longitudinal G stayed at or below -0.15 for at least one second from over 15 km/h.
Launch: longitudinal G stayed at or above 0.15 for at least one second, starting from under 5 km/h.

The grip envelope plots average speed in each corner against the peak lateral G you held in that corner. The upper edge of the cloud is your friction envelope: how hard you can actually lean at each speed. If your logger doesn't have an IMU, we estimate centripetal G from speed × heading rate on the GPS track and mark the section as GPS-estimated.

ANOMALY Free spins & suspected falls

Detection thresholds i

A free spin is a moment where the wheel reports a speed but GPS shows it didn't actually move. Most are pickup tests. A fall is a free spin that happens after you were already riding at speed: the wheel was rolling on the ground, then suddenly its reported speed disagreed with GPS and the ground speed crashed. Tune the thresholds above if your wheel triggers too many false positives or misses real events.

EVIDENCE Efficiency

Energy use over time (median per group)
Power-draw distribution (trip count by avg-W band)

Energy per distance (Wh/km, computed from logged power or voltage×current) and distance per battery percent (km/%). The histogram shows how hard you typically push the wheel: most riders sit in a narrow average-power band with a long tail toward steep climbs / sprints.

EVIDENCE Motor: speed vs current

Avg speed vs avg current (one dot per trip, old → new)
Current efficiency over time (amps per unit of speed)

Top: each dot is one trip, average moving speed against average (positive) current, coloured from oldest (blue) to newest (red). If recent dots sit above older ones at the same speed, the drivetrain is drawing more amps than it used to. Bottom: median amps per km/h per group, a speed-independent view of the same drift.

EVIDENCE+WEATHER Thermal

How much hotter the wheel got WEATHER (per ride, vs power output)
Heat-up per ride, tracked over time (median °C above ambient, per group)

For every ride we compute how much hotter the board got compared to the air around you — the temperature rise. Hot motors mean more electrical losses, more cooling pressure on the pack, and a higher risk of thermal cutoff on long climbs.

Top chart: each dot is one ride. X is the average power the wheel pulled, Y is the peak temperature rise. Dots drift from blue (oldest) to red (newest). If the recent (red) dots sit above the older (blue) ones at the same power level, the wheel is running hotter for the same work — a sign of degraded cooling or pack aging.

Bottom chart: the median rise per group across time. A flat line means consistent thermal behaviour; a drifting-up line means it's getting hotter to do the same ride.

EVIDENCE Battery health: voltage sag

Effective internal resistance over time (median per group, mΩ)
Voltage droop over the trip (% sag from peak, lower is healthier)

Two independent battery-aging proxies. Effective IR is the slope of voltage against current using only short-timestep deltas (so SoC drift across the trip cancels out). Voltage droop is the gap between the pack's peak resting voltage and its lowest loaded voltage during the same ride — naturally widens as the pack ages.
